About Me

My interests broadly encompass how animals respond to environments, and if those responses (behavioral and physiological traits) are adaptive. Extreme environments act as natural laboratories for the studies of adpatation, with alpine mountain tops such as the Trans-Himalayas of Ladakh posing some of the most unique and harshest environments to animals that live there. During my PhD, I studied behavioral adaptations of pikas to alpine stressors of cold and seasonal resource abundance in the Changthang Biotic province of Ladakh.

For my post-doctoral research, I am switching gears and exploring more mechanistic questions with respect to physiological adaptations of animal dormancy. I am studying how different groups of animals can use periods of dormancy to effectively save energy on thermoregulation (Torpor in Hummingbirds) or bridge unfavourable seasons (Diapause in insects). To address questions at both scales, I use a combination of field experiments, comparative phylogenetics and several Omics appproaches.

I have always been fascinated by insects since my childhood and am considering working on them in the future since they have the potential to address large-scale questions given the ease of maintaining laboratory populations and their small genome sizes.

Projects

Evolution of burrowing and associated behavioral traits in Lagomorphs

Closely related species of Lagomorphs are known to have varied lifestyles (construction of burrows, use of burrows of other animals, living in forms or rocks), sociality, and life history (r-selected, k-selected) traits. This diversity provides an excellent opportunity to study traits' evolution, adaptive potential, and genetic basis. Burrowing is an energetically expensive behavior that is thought to confer both thermoregulatory and anti-predatory advantages to animals. On the other hand, life history traits are generally subject to allometric scaling(species with smaller body sizes being more r- selected) and are affected directly by constraining climatic envelopes. While it has been speculated that burrowing species might be social (given their energetic constraints), they have also been suggested to have a r-selected life history, although this remains to be tested on a large scale. To test these hypotheses related to lifestyle, life history, and sociality, I constructed the first comprehensive dated Lagomorpha phylogeny and resolved the placement of O. ladacensis using whole genome sequencing. The ancestor of extant Ochotonids (13.75 mya) was inferred to be burrowing, while that of Leporids (18.2 mya) was inferred to live in forms or burrows of other species. I inferred that rock-dwelling behavior has evolved in pikas from a burrowing state in the clades of Conothoa and Pika. At current timescales, we did not find evidence that burrowing species live in cold/hot/variable climate regimes (thermoregulation hypothesis) or more open landscapes than species with other lifestyles (anti-predation hypothesis). We find little evidence that life history traits were driven by allometric scaling or directly by climatic envelopes. We find strong support for the burrowing —sociality—fecundity triangle where burrowing species were social and had r-selected life histories as opposed to rock-dwelling species. This supports the hypothesis that burrowing is energetically expensive and associated with life history and sociality. While we failed to understand if burrowing is an adaptation at current timescales on a large spatial scale, other ongoing experiments in the lab suggest that they offer significant thermoregulatory advantages in the summer (unpublished data). It is, however, likely that burrows offered thermoregulatory benefits to pikas historically during their evolution on the Qinghai-Tibetan Plateau (QTP) and helped animals to thermoregulate effectively during the glacial cycles of the Pleistocene.

Species distributions and comparative historical demography of sympatric pikas of the Tibetan Marginal mountains

Traits that are adaptations have fitness consequences and, therefore, influence effective population sizes or species distribution ranges. The ancestor of extant pikas evolved on the Qinghai-Tibetan Plateau (QTP) and was a burrower that was already adapted to cold before diversifying and migrating off the Tibetan plateau. Assuming that burrows provide better thermoregulatory advantages against cold and warm temperatures (when compared to rock crevices), I expected that effective population sizes and distribution ranges of burrowing species would not show large fluctuations through the Pleistocene compared to rock-dwelling species. The elevational shift hypothesis (supported by fossil records) suggests that pikas would be restricted to high elevations during the LIG (Last Interglacial; 130kya) warming, while expanding to lower elevations during the LGM (Last Glacial Maxima; 20kya) cooling (Galbreath et al., 2009; Ge et al., 2013). The niche expansion at LGM is likely to have facilitated adaptive introgression and outbreeding, helping populations recover from the LIG warming event. We used a species distribution modeling framework (SDMs: MAXENT) to reconstruct fundamental niches of the three alpine sympatric species (O. ladacensis - burrowing, O. nubrica - burrowing, and O. macrotis - rock-dwelling) at different periods and complemented it with historical demographic analysis from whole genomes (PSMCs) of pika species from the Changthang Biotic Province, Ladakh to gain insights into how suitable bioclimatic niches and effective population sizes have changed through the Pleistocene. Our species distribution modeling suggested that both rock-dwelling and burrowing species had the largest niches at LGM, which can be explained under the elevational shift model, with species distributions expanding to lower elevations at LGM and being restricted to higher elevations at LIG. However, the PSMCs for the two burrowing species of pikas suggested a sharp decline in effective population size post the LIG, similar to other range-restricted species such as Ochotona sikkimaria. This discordance in results was explained by examining the local paleoclimate and the geology of the Changthang biotic province from where the genomes were sampled. Given that the last local glacial maxima experienced by the region were weak, our results can be explained by invoking the elevation shift model coupled with change in vegetation communities and inbreeding post the LIG. While both SDMs and PSMCs have their drawbacks, discordance between the two can point to interesting biogeographic patterns or geology of regions from where animals were sampled. In this case, it points to the inaccuracy of distribution modeling projected back into the past using open-source paleoclimatic layers constructed using general circulation models (GCMs).
